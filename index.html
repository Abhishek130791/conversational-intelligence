The problem

The most valuable part of a sales call disappears the moment it ends.

Reps run dozens of calls a week. The signal that should drive the next move — who's really deciding, how much they opened up, what to send next — lives in someone's memory, or nowhere at all.

01

Insight stays trapped

What mattered on the call rarely makes it into the CRM in a form anyone can act on later.

02

Managers can't coach blind

You can't improve what you can't see. Reviewing calls by hand doesn't scale past a handful.

03

Follow-up slips

The window to act is hours, not days. By the time anyone writes notes, the moment's gone.

How it works

Six stages, one record.

A call lands, gets read for signal, and shows up on the rep's record with a verdict, follow-ups, evidence, and a drafted email — end to end, hands-off.

01

Capture

Pull the cloud recording once it's processed, keyed on a stable meeting ID.

02

Transcribe

Turn the recording into a speaker-labelled transcript.

03

Classify

An LLM reads the transcript and returns sub-scores and states — nothing more.

04

Derive

Code rolls the sub-scores into totals, buckets, and health flags. Deterministically.

05

Write

Upsert the structured result onto the meeting record in the CRM.

06

Notify

DM the rep who owned the call, with a link straight to the record.

Design decisions

The choices that make it trustworthy.

Anyone can ask a model for a score. The work was making that score deterministic, auditable, and honest about what it doesn't know.

// the model classifies, code does the math

No black box on the number that matters

The LLM only returns sub-scores and categorical states. Every aggregate — the total, the bucket, the account-health flag — is computed in code. The score is something you can re-derive and defend, not something a model felt.

// same call, same score, every time

Deterministic by construction

Scoring runs at temperature 0 with a fixed seed on a model that honours both. Reasoning models that silently ignore temperature were ruled out for scoring — determinism isn't negotiable when managers coach off the result.

// two lenses, one schema

Sales and post-sale ask different questions

Pre-sale calls get an engagement score. Post-sale calls get a readiness and account-health read instead — because once they've bought, "engagement" is the wrong question. The output schema stays frozen; only the prompt focus and weights change per call type.

// absent means absent

It never invents what wasn't said

If a budget was never mentioned, the field comes back null — never a plausible-looking guess. The evidence zone on the record lets a rep check any signal against what was actually said.

The rep experience

Two minutes instead of twenty.

A Slack DM hits. Glance at the verdict. If it needs action, open the record, grab the drafted email, and follow up while it's still fresh.

What the data said

Talk-time doesn't predict the deal. Authority and disclosure do.

Before settling the scoring rubric, I went back through closed calls to see what actually separated wins from losses — and the obvious metric turned out to be noise.

≈ not predictive

How much the rep talked, on its own, didn't separate won calls from lost ones — so it earns no weight in the score.

authority + depth

Whether a real decision-maker was in the room, and how much the prospect opened up, were the signals that tracked with outcomes. The rubric is built around those.
